Part 1

ISO 19160-2 Explained

Principles · Good Practice · Governance

The ISO 19160 Addressing Series

  • ISO 19160 is the international standard family for addressing physical-world objects.
  • ISO 19160-1 — Conceptual model: address classes, components, relationships
  • ISO 19160-2 — Assigning and maintaining addresses (this analysis)
  • ISO 19160-3 — Address data quality measures
  • ISO 19160-4 — Postal address components (UPU S42 profile)
  • ISO 19160-2:2023 defines how countries should assign addresses and govern them over time.
  • It contains 32 mandatory requirements and 14 recommendations across two conformance classes.

ISO 19160-2: Two Conformance Classes

  • Good Practice (Clause 7) — WHAT addresses look like and HOW they are assigned
    • Objectives, context, formal address reference system (19160-1 profile)
    • Sustainable assignment method, piloted before rollout
    • Physical identifiers (signage) and address data / ADMS
  • Governance Framework (Clause 8) — WHO manages addressing and THROUGH WHAT processes
    • National strategy, policies, legal mandates
    • Stakeholder identification, responsibilities, resourcing
    • Eight lifecycle processes from initiation to data distribution
  • Both classes must be met to claim ISO 19160-2 conformance.

Good Practice — Addressing Principles (7.2.1)

  • Sustainable assignment — numbering survives densification without renumbering
  • Pilot before rollout — test method in real Thromdes before national scale
  • Device independence — navigation works without GPS or connectivity
  • No personal information — addresses must not embed owner/occupant names
  • Address classes — formal, provisional, institutional types with suitable components
  • Unambiguous object binding — each address links to one physical object
  • Permanent digital record — UAI or equivalent identifier for every address
  • Event-driven updates — database changes when physical address changes

Good Practice — Address Data Principles (7.2.2)

  • Data mirrors physical world — GIS geometry matches real entrances and parcels
  • Interoperability — exchange with cadastre, postal operator, emergency services
  • Data maintenance — stewardship, versioning, quality assurance processes
  • ADMS — authoritative Address Data Management System (database + GIS + workflows)
  • ISO 19160-1 data model — standardized classes, aliases, lifecycle attributes
  • ISO 19160-4 / UPU S42 — postal routing template co-developed with Bhutan Post
  • ISO 19160-3 quality — completeness, accuracy, timeliness metrics and reporting
  • API sharing — public or controlled access for navigators and service providers

Governance — Strategy & Stakeholders (8.1–8.2)

  • National addressing strategy — phased rollout, KPIs, long-term vision
  • Binding policies — who approves what, at which level
  • Stakeholder register — MoIT, DHS, Thromdes, Post, NLCS, BSB, EMS, utilities, citizens
  • Responsibilities — RACI matrix with SLAs and measurable KPIs
  • Legal mandates — empowering Acts, Thromde by-laws, statutory naming authority
  • Resourcing — budgets, FTE address officers, signage funds, GIS licences
  • Sustainable funding — operational maintenance beyond initial project phase

Governance — Eight Lifecycle Processes (8.3)

  • 1. Initiation — trigger from building permit, subdivision, or street creation
  • 2. Consultation — community input on street naming (recommended)
  • 3. Propose values — municipality submits proposed name and number
  • 4. Approval — formal accept/reject with authority level and appeal path
  • 5. Announcement — official gazette, web portal, change log
  • 6. Physical communication — install BSB33-compliant signage on schedule
  • 7. Update dataset — ADMS updated within days of approval (not batch-only)
  • 8. Distribution — push to Bhutan Post, EMS, navigators, open-data consumers

Part 2

Gap Analysis

Bhutan City Addressing Guideline vs ISO 19160-2

Executive Summary

  • Bhutan proposal is a strong urban operational manual — street naming, 10 m numbering, BSB33 signage.
  • Relative to ISO 19160-2, it reads as an implementation design guide, not a complete addressing infrastructure standard.
  • Does NOT currently conform to either ISO conformance class.
  • Good Practice: 5 met · 4 partial · 6 not met (of 15 requirements)
  • Governance: 0 met · 7 partial · 10 not met (of 17 requirements)
  • Overall: 5/32 requirements fully met · 11 partial · 16 not met

Good Practice — Bhutan Strengths

  • Objectives (Req 1) — Six clear service-delivery objectives in Section 2
  • Context-sensitive assignment — Valley morphology, 10 m hybrid model
  • Sustainable numbering (Req 6) — Odd/even, 10 m intervals, reserved numbers, suffixes
  • Physical identifiers (Req 5) — Extensive BSB33 bilingual signage (Sections 11–12)
  • Device independence (Req 8) — Visible signs + distance-encoded numbers
  • No personal information (Req 9) — Prohibits naming streets after living persons
  • Comparable to Korea KAIS, AS/NZS 4819, and France BAN for physical standards

Good Practice — Critical Gaps

  • Theme A: No ISO 19160-1 address reference system or address classes (Req 3)
    • No parent/child unit model; Annex I missing; UAI marked [FUTURE]
  • Theme B: No ADMS spec, interoperability, data licence, or quality framework
    • Bi-annual batch sync instead of event-driven updates
  • Theme C: No pilot before national rollout (Req 7)
  • Theme D: Inconsistent address components; postcode deferred

Governance — Assessment

  • Verdict: Does NOT conform to GovernanceFramework conformance class.
  • Partial: Stakeholder list (Sections 13, 15), institutional role sketch, BSB33 integration.
  • Missing: Legal mandates, budgets, RACI/SLA, and all eight lifecycle processes.
  • 0 of 8 ISO lifecycle processes are defined in the Bhutan guideline.
  • Signage standards exist (what signs look like) but not process (when/who installs after approval).
  • Bi-annual DHS reporting replaces event-driven dataset update and distribution.

Cross-Cutting Findings

  • Urban-only scope — ISO applies to all addressable objects including rural and highways
  • Missing Annex I — unit numbering deferred but annex does not exist
  • Postcode marked "future" despite examples using 12001, 21101
  • No rules for street rename, number reassignment, or address retirement
  • Document quality issues — placeholders, section numbering errors, typography conflicts
  • No conformance statement mapping to ISO 19160-2 requirement URIs

Structural Recommendations (P1)

  • Reframe as Bhutan National Addressing Good Practice — Urban Profile targeting ISO 19160-2
  • Add Address Reference System chapter profiling ISO 19160-1: classes, components, lifecycle
  • Make UAI mandatory from day one; link to NLCS parcel IDs
  • Define all eight governance processes with flowcharts, SLAs, and RACI per step
  • Complete Annex I for unit/floor/child addressing
  • Mandate 12–18 month pilot in Thimphu + Phuentsholing with KPIs before national rollout

Data, Legal & Implementation (P2–P4)

  • Co-develop ISO 19160-4 / UPU S42 postal template with Bhutan Post; finalize postcode
  • Specify national ADMS: GIS platform, API, exchange format, metadata, data licence
  • Adopt ISO 19160-3 quality measures with annual public quality report
  • Publish National Addressing Strategy; legal review for Addressing Act or Thromde Act amendment
  • Budget and capacity plan: address officers, signage, GIS licences per Thromde
  • Consultation SOP for street naming; event-driven ADMS updates within 7 days of approval

Proposed Revised Document Structure

  • 0. Normative References (ISO 19160-1, -2, -3, -4; BSB33; UPU S42)
  • 1. Context and Objectives
  • 2. Definitions (ISO 19160-1 aligned)
  • 3. Address Reference System (Bhutan Urban Profile)
  • 4–6. Street naming, building numbering, special cases
  • 7. Address Format and UAI (UAI mandatory)
  • 8. Signage Standards (Sections 11–12)
  • 9. Address Data and ADMS Specification
  • 10. Governance, Processes, and Institutional Roles
  • 11. Pilot and Evaluation Framework
  • Annex I: Unit/Child Addressing
  • Annex II: ISO 19160-2 Conformance Mapping

Conclusion

  • Bhutan guideline is above average for urban thoroughfare addressing — especially 10 m numbering and BSB33 signage.
  • Five blocking gaps prevent ISO 19160-2 conformance:
    • No ISO 19160-1 address reference system or address classes
    • No governance lifecycle processes (0 of 8 defined)
    • No ADMS specification, data licensing, or interoperability architecture
    • UAI and postcode deferred though ISO treats them as infrastructure core
    • Missing Annex I and incomplete draft status
  • With Priority 1–2 actions, Bhutan can pursue conformance while retaining its valley addressing model and Dzongkha–English signage framework.
